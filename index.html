Do cats know their names?

Louie, Wearing a nametag

Image generated by Midjourney

According to PetMD¹ cats don't know their own names as long as you use positive reinforcement. They can also learn the names of your other pets and can tell when they are being talked to or if someone or something else is being talked to.

The best way to teach your cat its name is to associate it with good things. Try saying its name before giving it treats, playing with it, or petting it. Try to avoid saying its name when doing things it may not like, such as putting it in its carrier or giving them a bath.

Essential tips to raise healthy cats

Brittany, playing with some yarn on a string

Image generated by Midjourney

An article by the Valley West & Elk Valley Veterinary Hospital² gives the impression that there are seven vital steps to raising a health and happy cat. Paying attention to their diet, particularly giving them good wet food, is the vital first step. Your cat always needs water available as well, but they get most of their water from their food. It's important to introduce wet food early, because they can get too used to dry food and turn their nose up at the healthier food.

A second vital step is to make sure your cat has a safe place to go if they ever feel threatend or stressed, and that their litter box is in such a place. This includes giving each cat you care for its own seperate litter box, and avoiding lidded litter boxes.

The third thing VWEVVH lists is not having too many cats. Cats are territorial, and while they can be social, they typically want lots of space for themselves. The article says that two is a good maximum, but I disagree and say three is a good number.

It is recomended to enrich your cats' environment fourth, in order to cut down on vet visits. At the same time, they list seeing the vet regularly as their fifth item. This means providing lots of toys and climbing spaces, and scratching posts; that way, your cat gets the exercise they need without being destructive. And when you do go to the vet, it should be to get the vaccinations they need, and hopefully not for injuries they have sustained by being let outdoors because the indoors isn't enriching enough.

Article item six is brushing. While your cat may lick themselves clean, this can cause them to swallow their loose fur and cause gross hairballs. These are not only a pain to clean up after your cat has hacked them up, but they can hurt the cat while it's trying to dislodge it! Brushing the cat regularly to remove excess fur can prevent this, and if the cat isn't cooperative you can assosiate brushing with positive things like giving them treats.

Last on their list is to spay or neuter your cat. They say that a cat in heat is very uncomfortable, so spaying or neutering the cat can help prevent that discomfort. It also keeps cats from becoming overpopulated, because too many cats have to be put down since there's not enough people to care for them.

Why does my cat attack me?

Charlie, Ready to pounce

Image generated by Midjourney

I'm going to answer this one from personal experience. The personality of a cat is as varied as the personality of a person. Some people are just spiteful jerks while others enjoy playful banter, and some cats are just hunters by nature. The primary motiviation for a cat to pounce on you is their hunting instinct, which for some cats is more pronounced than others.

While hunting and pouncing can be seen as a form of play, occasionally you may suffer cat attacks of other varieties. Aggressive clawings when your cat is in a small space, possibly trying to get away from you, are an indication that you are bothering the cat and not giving them enough space. It can be a sign that there is no safe place for them to go, or that you have been hurting your cat somehow.

A cat that bites hard is unhappy in similar but different ways. It does not want to be touched, moved, or sought after. It probably feels smothered. Try giving a biting cat more independance, and more hiding spaces. In a cluttered house, this can also be a sign that the cat is stressed. However, playful bites, whether they break skin or not, are not meant to hurt you, and they indicate that your cat is simply bored. Try getting some more toys, and interacting with your cat more. I suggest letting them sniff whatever you are handling, such as food or phones, when they come to investigate.

How to throw a birthday party for your cat

Collin, he's wearing a silly hat!

Image generated by Midjourney

Cats don't really understand the concept of a birthday. They may be vaguely aware of seasons passing, but they don't mark the days on a calendar the same way humans do. But if you want to throw a party for your cat, the best way is to not disturb the cat's routine.

Start by inviting only people who the cat is familiar with. You can invite other pets as well, if you know the cat deals well with those pets and socialization. But do not invite too many people and pets. Your cat is still territorial, and too many beings infringing on your cat's home can really stress them out. A small party is a good party.

Do not over decorate. Your cat won't understand that the party is for them, and too many decorations can make their environment cluttered and unfamiliar, which also stresses them out.

Get them a healthy but tasty treat. Don't feed your cat regular cake that a human would eat. You can have a regular cake to share with your human guests, but cats are carnivourous. A large dish of salmon or other meats is a good treat for a cat to eat on their birthday.
